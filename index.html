Beginners' Guide to Caviar

Classifications of Caviar

There are two classifications of caviar: fresh and farmed.


Fresh Caviar


Fresh caviar includes Beluga, Osetra, and Sevruga varieties. They are from sturgeon fish in the Caspian and Black Sea.



Farmed Caviar


Farmed caviar includes Ossetra, Hackleback, and Paddlefish varieties. This caviar typically comes from American fisheries.



Varieties of Caviar

Caviar mainly comes from sturgeon fish.





Besides sturgeon, caviar can also come from lumpfish, salmon, trout, whitefish, bowfin, or smelt fish. There are caviar varieties to fit any budget. Masiago caviar (from smelt fish) is orange in color and small in size, typically used as garnish on sushi and very inexpensive in price. Beluga, Ossetra, and Sevruga varieties range in color from gray to black and range in taste from mild to briny (fishy). They also range in price from $40 up.



Food Pairings

Caviar can be served in a multitude of ways. You can eat it for breakfast on eggs benedict. You can serve it on top of sushi for lunch or dinner. You can enjoy it by the spoonful. Or, you can have it as an appetizer on crackers or blinis (mini pancakes) with creme fraiche, fresh fish, and a sprig of fresh dill.


Little Known Facts

  • The most expensive caviar is called, "Almas", and comes from wild albino sturgeon in the Caspian Sea aged between 60 and 100 years old. The price for this caviar can reach $20,000.
  • Beluga caviar does not come from Beluga whales. It comes from Beluga sturgeon found in the Caspian and Black Seas.
  • Fresh caviar is expensive because many species of sturgeon that the roe (fish eggs) come from are endangered.
  • To improve the taste of caviar, use a wooden or Mother of Pearl spoon instead of metal. Metal transfers a metallic taste to the delicate caviar.
